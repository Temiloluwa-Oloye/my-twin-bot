Conventional handwritten digit recognition systems often rely on computationally intensive models, limiting their deployment on portable, low-cost devices. This paper presents a TinyML-based system for real-time handwritten digit recognition designed specifically to assist visually impaired users. Leveraging a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) deployed on a Raspberry Pi, the system delivers accurate digit recognition with both visual and audio feedback, enabling independent identification of hand-written digits encountered in everyday activities. The integrated solution combines a Pi camera module, a 3.5-inch display, and an audio speaker, resulting in a compact, portable, and user-friendly device. The methodology involves training the CNN on a curated dataset and optimizing it for edge deployment using TinyML techniques, ensuring low-latency and energy-efficient operation. Experimental results demonstrate the system's capability for efficient and reliable digit recognition, highlighting its potential to enhance accessibility and empower visually impaired individuals through affordable, real-time assistive technology.1. IntroductionTiny Machine Learning (TinyML) refers to the deployment of machine learning models on resource-constrained devices, typically embedded systems with low-power processors and limited memory [1]. By performing inference directly on these devices, TinyML enables real time decision-making without constant connectivity to cloud servers, improving privacy, reducing latency, and conserving bandwidth [2].Handwritten digit recognition has been a central research topic in machine learning and computer vision for decades, particularly in applications aimed at assisting visually impaired individuals. Traditional approaches, such as back-propagation networks, have been widely applied for this task [3]. Support Vector Machines (SVMs) have also been explored, though they often underperform compared to Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) due to dataset size limitations [4]. Comparatively, Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) achieve higher accuracy in digit classification [5]. CNN are highly effective for image recognition due to their ability to automatically extract hierarchical features from raw pixel data. They have been successfully applied in object detection, facial recognition, and medical imaging tasks [6], [7]. CNN process images through multiple layers, including convolutional, pooling, and fully connected layers, where lower-level features often provide high discriminative power [8]. Multi-Attention CNN (MA-CNN) further improve fine-grained recognition by incorporating attention mechanisms, channel grouping, and part classification sub-networks, achieving state-of-the-art performance without requiring manual annotations [9], [10].Development of assistive technology for the old and persons with desirability is an active area of research [11]. Recent advancements in TinyML have enabled the development of assistive technologies for the visually impaired. Navigation aids using touchscreens typically provide vibration, audio feedback, or both, with audio feedback offering faster and more efficient responses for many users [12], [13]. Previous works, while effective, either rely on computationally intensive models or lack real-time portability and multimodal feedback suitable for daily use, indicating a clear gap in accessible, portable, and efficient handwritten digit recognition systems.This paper presents a TinyML-based system for handwritten digit recognition specifically designed to aid visually impaired individuals. The proposed system leverages a CNN model deployed on a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B, integrating a Pi camera module, a 3.5-inch display, and an audio speaker to deliver both visual and audio feedback in real-time. The key contributions are the development of a lightweight, portable, and low-cost TinyML solution, the integration of multimodal feedback mechanisms for enhanced accessibility, and the demonstration of real-time performance and accuracy suitable for practical use by visually impaired users.2. Related WorkAlajlan and Ibrahim provided a comprehensive review of the TinyML revolution, highlighting its capability to enable deep learning inference on ultra-low-power devices [14]. Shifting from cloud-based processing to local device processing significantly reduces latency, conserves bandwidth, and enhances data privacy. The theoretical foundation and benefits of TinyML such as energy efficiency and low cost were established.Lamaakal et al. introduced a Tiny Deep Learning (TinyDL) model specifically designed for recognizing Arabic numerals and letters through air handwriting [15]. Utilizing an Arduino Nano 33 BLE Sense equipped with inertial measurement unit (IMU) sensors, a high recognition accuracy of 97.5% was achieved by capturing motion data rather than visual data. The complexity of Arabic script was effectively addressed without requiring a camera; the model relies on gestures in the air, differing from recognizing static written text.Saha et al. developed a low-cost system for controlling appliances using static hand gestures, employing an ESP32-CAM module [16]. The CNN approach classifies gestures with over 92% accuracy, proving the viability of vision-based TinyML on resource-constrained microcontrollers. However, the solution focuses on home automation rather than providing descriptive feedback for visually impaired users and is optimized for specific hand shapes rather than digit recognition.Chittepu et al. explored integrating TinyML with voice assistants to enhance user interfaces in healthcare and smart homes [17]. The advantages of processing voice commands locally were highlighted, though current TinyML voice models face challenges with noise interference. The solution emphasizes audio feedback mechanisms but focuses on speech processing rather than computer vision tasks for reading assistance.Kunhoth et al. proposed "VisualAid+," a wearable assistive system combining a Raspberry Pi for object detection with a server-based module for image captioning [18]. The system employs a hierarchical approach where person detection runs locally (81.15% accuracy), but complex narration relies on a remote server, introducing potential latency and limiting offline usability. Furthermore, it is designed for general object detection rather than high-precision handwritten digit recognition.In this paper, a TinyML-based handwritten digit recognition system is specifically designed to aid visually impaired individuals. Unlike cloud-reliant systems, the proposed solution utilizes a CNN model deployed directly on a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B to perform inference entirely on the edge, ensuring complete data privacy and offline functionality. Integrating visual verification and distinct audio feedback offers a robust, multimodal, and fully portable assistive tool.3. General System Design3.1 Hardware ComponentsThe proposed system features a CNN model deployed on a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B, interfaced with peripherals for image capture, visual output, and audio feedback. The Pi camera module captures images of handwritten digits, which are fed into the CNN model for inference. The model predicts digits with high accuracy. Recognized digits are displayed in real-time on a 3.5-inch display for visual feedback, and simultaneously communicated audibly through an integrated speaker to assist visually impaired users.Raspberry Pi 4 Model B: Represents a significant advancement in embedded computing, featuring a Broadcom BCM2711 quad-core Cortex-A72 64-bit SoC at 1.5 GHz with up to 8 GB SDRAM [12]. Key features include dual micro-HDMI ports, Gigabit Ethernet, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth 5.0, USB ports, and 40 GPIO pins. The GPIO pins provide power (3.3V, 5V, GND), input/output functions (I2C, UART), and special functions like PWM and SPI.Interfacing Peripherals: The Pi camera module connects to the Camera Serial Interface (CSI) port for high-speed data transfer. It is configured via the Raspbian OS to capture images. The 3.5-inch display interfaces with appropriate GPIO pins, requiring precise pin selection to avoid operational disruptions. The audio speaker connects directly to the standard audio jack to provide auditory feedback.3.2 Software ComponentsConvolutional Neural Network (CNN) Model: The core of the system, trained using a dataset of handwritten digits, and optimized for efficient inference on resource-constrained devices like the Raspberry Pi.TensorFlow Lite: A lightweight version of TensorFlow optimized for mobile and embedded devices, chosen for its low-latency inference on the Raspberry Pi 4 Model B [13]. Setting up the system involves installing Raspbian OS, necessary libraries, configuring TensorFlow Lite/GPIO pins, and testing.Python Programming Language: Used primarily due to its versatility and extensive support for machine learning frameworks and Raspberry Pi libraries.Libraries: Essential libraries utilized include TensorFlow, Keras, NumPy, Pandas, Matplotlib, Subprocess, Time module, PIL, Pygame, and OpenCV.3.3 The DatasetThe Modified National Institute of Standards and Technology (MNIST) dataset is utilized, originating from NIST Special Databases 3 and 1 [20]. It contains 70,000 $28\times28$-pixel grayscale images of handwritten digits (0-9), split into 60,000 training images and 10,000 testing images [3]. The dataset is balanced (~6,000 training samples per class) and requires minimal preprocessing; pixel values are normalized to $[0,1]$ by dividing by 255 to enhance model convergence [3].3.4 The Recognition ProcessThe camera module is initialized and shows a brief preview for framing. The captured image is processed by the CNN model for inference. The predicted digit is then displayed on the screen and conveyed as audio feedback.3.5 Selection of Sounds Corresponding to Predicted DigitsDistinct sound tones represent each digit (0-9) for clarity. Higher pitches correspond to higher digits, while lower pitches represent lower digits. Tone duration is optimized for responsiveness, and the timbre is pleasant, enabling accurate interpretation even in noisy environments.3.6 CNN Model Architecture and Training ProcessThe CNN handles pixel-level image data using multiple layers [22]:Input Layer: Represents the raw image dimensions forming a three-dimensional grid [24].Convolutional Layers: Perform feature extraction using filters to detect patterns; non-linear activation functions (ReLU) are applied [25].Max Pooling Layers: Downsample feature maps to improve efficiency, reduce overfitting, and preserve features [1], [25].Flatten Layer: Converts multi-dimensional maps into a 1D vector [1], [26].Dense (Fully Connected) Layers: Classify extracted features into categories using weighted sums and activation functions [5].Softmax Activation Layer: Used for multi-class classification, normalizing output scores into probability distributions [20], [27].4. Results and Discussions4.1 Evaluation MetricsSystem performance was evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score:$$Accuracy = \frac{TP+TN}{TP+FP+TN+FN}$$Where $TP$ (True Positives) are correctly identified digits, $FP$ (False Positives) are incorrect positive predictions, $FN$ (False Negatives) are missed digits, and $TN$ (True Negatives) are correctly identified as not belonging to a class.$$Precision = \frac{TP}{TP+FP}$$$$Recall = \frac{TP}{TP+FN}$$$$F1-Score = 2 \times \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}$$4.2 Model Loss and Accuracy EvaluationThe CNN model achieved an overall accuracy of 98.83% on the test dataset. Over 20 training epochs, the loss decreased steadily from 0.0675 to 0.0366, while accuracy increased from 97.9% to 98.8%. The validation loss remained low and stable (~0.06), and validation accuracy stayed high (~98%), demonstrating that the model generalized well without overfitting.4.3 Precision, Recall, and F1-Score EvaluationThese metrics showcase the high accuracy and reliability of the system across all classes. High precision indicates minimal false positives, critical for aiding visually impaired individuals. High recall demonstrates minimal false negatives. The impressive F1-score performance in classes 1, 5, 8, and 9 suggests the system excels at distinguishing digits with intricate or similar features.Experiments under varying lighting conditions showed consistent performance, implying that the preprocessing and feature extraction phases play a major role in system stability. The model is not unduly sensitive to small changes in input quality; however, incorrect predictions were observed mostly under dim lighting, which is the system's major limitation. Misclassifications usually happen between visually similar digits due to innate form resemblance rather than inefficient models. Ultimately, the outcomes validate the feasibility of deploying the system on resource-constrained platforms, delivering timely and accurate recognition without excessive computational overhead.5. ConclusionThis project successfully achieved its objectives of creating an innovative solution to assist individuals with visual impairments in recognizing handwritten digits. Integrating a CNN model with a Raspberry Pi 4 Model B, a Pi camera, a 3.5-inch display, and an audio speaker provided comprehensive multimodal feedback. The system achieved high accuracy rates and low latency during real-time operation. Comparative analysis highlighted its portability, affordability, and real-time processing capabilities.
